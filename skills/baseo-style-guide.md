# baseo-style-guide

A creation reference. Writers consult this skill before drafting a BASEO blog post and during writing to resolve "how do I phrase this?" questions. Editors reference it when sending feedback. The goal is consistency across writers — a reader should not be able to guess which writer wrote which post.

## When to use this skill

- A writer has a brief (cluster + heading structure + per-heading guidelines) and is about to start drafting.
- A writer is stuck on tone, intro, or how to weave BASEO into the body.
- An editor is sending feedback and wants to cite the rule, not relitigate the criterion.
- Anyone creating copy adjacent to blog content: landing pages, ads, email, social. The full guide applies to long-form blogs; the **Voice rules** section applies universally.

## What this skill expects you to have loaded

Before applying this guide, the skill (or writer) must have read **`baseo-context`**. That file defines:

- Who reads BASEO content (personas B1–B5 and anti-personas).
- What services exist (canonical names + aliases) and what's out of scope.
- Which pains map to which services.
- The Approved claims library and proof rules.
- Funnel stage definitions and CTA expectations.
- The full list of banned clichés, agency-speak, trust-killers, and anti-angles.
- The pricing/package disclosure policy.
- Canonical BASEO URLs.

This style guide does NOT redefine any of those. It defines how to WRITE — voice, structure, intros, H2s, paragraph rhythm, deal-math sentences, service integration, internal linking, visual cadence, closes. When voice meets context, context wins.

## The voice in one paragraph

You are an operator who happens to know SEO, talking to another operator. The reader closes real estate deals for a living, has been burned by at least one agency, and can smell a pitch from the subject line. He wants the bottom line, the numbers that matter (leads, cost per lead, cost per deal), and proof from someone in his exact niche. Write short. Write blunt. Show the math. Show the work. Never lecture, never hype, never use the words his last agency used. Respect is earned by specificity: name the keyword, name the cost per click, name the month the leads showed up.

This is the Ahrefs-style practitioner voice with two tilts: (1) everything translates to deals and dollars, because that's how the reader thinks; (2) teach the approach, not the operations manual. We explain WHAT good investor SEO looks like and WHY it works, in enough depth to prove we know the niche cold. We do NOT publish HOW we execute it (the tools, the exact counts, the internal workflow). Mentioning that BASEO does the work is enough; spelling out the how just hands the playbook to competitors. The reader who wants to DIY the concept was never going to be a client; the one who values his time sees the expertise and calls us.

## Voice rules

### 1. Short sentences. Short paragraphs.

Most sentences fit on one line. Most paragraphs are 1–3 sentences. One-sentence paragraphs are valid and powerful. The reader writes short blunt texts; mirror that rhythm.

**Good**:
> Your competitor closes half the deals you do. But he ranks #1 in your city.
>
> That gap isn't talent. It's 18 months of city pages you didn't build.

**Bad**:
> In the increasingly competitive landscape of real estate investing, establishing a robust online presence has become an essential component of any comprehensive lead generation strategy for forward-thinking investors.

The bad example tells the reader nothing and sounds like the agency that burned him. Strip it.

### 2. Take a stance. Have opinions.

The reader can detect waffle, and waffle reads as "agency hiding something." If you have a position, state it.

**Good**:
> Carrot is a website platform. It is not an SEO strategy. The default template content is shared across thousands of investor sites, and Google can't decide which one of you to rank — so it ranks none of you well.

**Bad**: "Some experts suggest template websites may have certain limitations, depending on your goals."

### 3. Deal math before adjectives

When you can quantify, quantify — and when you can convert the number to deals or dollars, convert it. "Significant savings" is nothing. "$161 per organic lead vs $250+ per PPC lead" is something. "$7,500 per deal on paid vs $500–$2,000 organic" is the sentence the reader does napkin math on.

**Good**:
> At a 4% close rate and $250 per PPC lead, you're paying $7,500 in marketing per deal. Mature organic brings that to $500–$2,000.

**Bad**: "Organic leads are dramatically more cost-effective over time."

If you don't have the number, cite a source that does (per `baseo-context` § Claims policy), or cut the claim.

### 4. Italics for one word, not three

Use italics to flag a single load-bearing word. Don't italicize phrases or whole sentences.

**Good**: "He didn't increase his ad budget. He *stopped* renting attention."

**Bad**: "*This is really important for your lead generation strategy.*"

### 5. Bold for nouns, almost never for verbs

Bold the term that names a concept, service, or step. Don't bold for emphasis — that's italics' job.

**Good**: "The hierarchy that works: **city landing pages** as primary lead generators, **seller-situation pages** for high-intent long-tail, **trust content** to lift conversion."

**Bad**: "You **really should** build city pages if you **want more** leads."

### 6. No em-dashes

Em-dashes (—) are banned in editorial output. They're a signature pattern of AI-generated text. Use natural-language alternatives:

| Em-dash use case | Replacement |
|---|---|
| Joining two complete clauses | Period + new sentence |
| Parenthetical aside | Comma + comma, or parentheses |
| Introducing a list or definition | Colon |
| Soft pause / breath | Comma |
| Conjunction (because, so, but) | The actual conjunction word |

En-dashes (–) for numeric ranges like "$2,500–$4,500" are fine and standard.

### 7. Empathy with the burned operator, not the beginner

The reader isn't confused about his business. He's exhausted by agencies. Acknowledge the burn without wallowing in it, then move to proof.

**Good**: "If your last agency's reports were full of impressions and 'keyword movement' but never the word leads, you already know what to look for in the next one."

**Bad**: "SEO can seem overwhelming, but don't worry — we'll guide you through every step of your journey."

### 8. Plain-language translation at first use (the jargon rule)

The reader's SEO literacy is low-to-moderate. Every technical term gets a plain operator translation in ten words or fewer, inline, at first use. After that, use the term freely.

**Good**: "Schema markup (labels that tell Google exactly what your page is) is missing on most investor sites."

**Good**: "Most of your AI traffic shows up as 'Direct' in Google Analytics because the attribution breaks. Carrot calls this dark traffic."

**Bad**: stopping the article for a 150-word definition paragraph. The reader skims past explainer blocks.

The banned agency-speak table in `baseo-context` § Banned clichés is mandatory here: leads not conversions, cost per deal not ROAS, "showing up on Google" not "organic visibility."

### 9. Never sound like the agency that burned him

Before submitting, read the draft once as the B1 persona. Any sentence that could have appeared in his previous agency's pitch deck ("trust the process," "results take time," "we're different," vague promises, jargon walls) gets cut or made specific. The fastest credibility signals: niche specificity (Carrot, probate, assignment fees), real numbers, and honest statements against self-interest ("if your market doesn't support the math, SEO is the wrong buy and the audit will say so").

## Persona voice adjustments

The core voice is constant. Four small dials per target persona (from the brief):

- **B1 Burned Investor**: lead with proof and accountability mechanics. Acknowledge the burn early. Heavier use of "in writing," "you keep it," "no contract." Never defensive about the industry; agree with his skepticism, then differentiate.
- **B2 PPC-Dependent**: numbers-first. Tables comparing CPL and cost per deal. Never tell him to kill PPC day one; the honest position is hybrid-then-shift (it's also the public FAQ position). Since BASEO manages PPC too, this honesty costs nothing and reads as credibility: we'd rather run his ads well while organic compounds than trash the channel.
- **B3 Invisible Veteran**: respect first. Reference his deal volume and offline reputation before naming the gap. Identity framing ("the name sellers hear about but can't find").
- **B4 Scaling Wholesaler**: most educational depth. He'll actually implement steps, so steps must be real. Build-vs-buy honesty: tell him what he can DIY and where DIY plateaus.
- **B5 AEO-attached briefs**: mechanism over hype. Always explain HOW AI search routes a seller (intent compression, citations) before any urgency claim. One urgency note max per article; the window argument loses power if shouted.

## Intro patterns

Every BASEO intro must hook the reader in under 80 words. Three valid templates. Pick one. Never lead with context, warm-up, or "in this article you'll learn..." as the first paragraph.

### Template A — Challenge a belief

State a common claim, then push back. Best for posts that reframe an assumption.

**BASEO example**:
> Most investors think their homepage is their most important page. It isn't even close.
>
> On the investor sites that actually pull leads, 60%+ of organic traffic lands on city pages. The homepage is where sellers go to check you're real, after a city page already caught them.

### Template B — Promise a concrete outcome

Lead with what the reader gets if they read on. Best for how-to and BOFU posts.

**BASEO example**:
> By the end of this post you'll know exactly what an SEO agency should hand you every month: the deliverables list, the report format, and the three numbers that matter. If your current agency can't show them, you'll know that too.

### Template C — Relatable scene or comparison

Lead with a frame that makes the problem visceral. Best for explainer / TOFU posts. For this audience, the strongest "analogies" are deal analogies and competitor scenes.

**BASEO example**:
> A seller in your city opened ChatGPT last night and typed "how do I sell my house fast for cash." ChatGPT answered with three companies. You weren't one of them.
>
> That conversation is happening every week, and the list it picks from is short.

### Banned intro openers

- "In today's [competitive / digital / fast-paced] real estate market..."
- "SEO is more important than ever..."
- "Have you ever wondered..."
- "Let's dive into..."
- "As a real estate investor, you know that..."
- Any paragraph that could front-load a different article on a different topic.

If your intro could open any post in the category, rewrite it.

## H2 and H3 patterns

H2s and H3s carry double duty: they make the article scannable for humans AND they capture keyword variations for SEO. Most of the keyword coverage from the cluster should land in these headings, not buried in body copy.

### H2 rules

**Rule #1 — Keyword coverage comes first.** The primary keyword (or a close variation) must appear in at least 60% of the H2s, naturally. This is the SEO floor and overrides any stylistic preference. If a format choice forces you to drop the keyword from an H2, change the format, not the keyword.

**Rule #2 — Length: 3–8 words target, up to 12 if it captures a real long-tail.** "How much does SEO for real estate investors cost per deal?" (10 words) earns its length if the long-tail has volume.

**Rule #3 — Preferred formats** (starting points, not rigid):

- **Question**: "How do motivated sellers actually search?" / "Is Carrot enough for SEO?"
- **Number + noun**: "The 5 seller-situation pages every investor site needs" / "3 numbers your SEO report must show"
- **Declarative with the keyword**: "SEO for real estate investors: the deal math"

Mix formats freely; the consistency that matters is keyword presence.

**Rule #4 — Stylistic H2s without keyword: 1–2 per article max**, and only after the keyword is already covered in earlier H2s.

### H3 rules

H3s capture **long-tail variations and specific sub-topics**. They're where lower-volume cluster keywords live and where PAA-style questions get answered.

- **Length**: up to 12 words. Often more descriptive than H2s.
- **Coverage rule**: across H2s + H3s combined, at least 60% of the keyword cluster should be represented in headings.

### Banned H2/H3 patterns

- Vague abstractions ("The bigger picture", "Going deeper", "What's next").
- Two-clause questions — split or reframe.
- Marketing-verb H2s ("Unlock more motivated seller leads", "Supercharge your investor SEO").
- Keyword-stuffed H3s that repeat the parent H2 with no new sub-topic.

## Paragraph rhythm

Target: average paragraph length 1–3 sentences. One-sentence paragraphs are encouraged for emphasis or transitions. Paragraphs longer than 4 sentences must justify themselves — usually they're doing two things and should split.

**Test**: if any paragraph fills more than 4 visual lines on a desktop blog layout, split it.

## Deal-math-first sentences (the signature pattern)

Every substantive claim should be backed by a number, a quote, or a link to a source — and where possible, translated into the reader's units: leads, cost per lead, cost per deal, deals per month.

Pattern: specific number → context → contrasting number or deal implication.

**Example**:
> One Florida client went from 3 organic leads a month to 28 in nine months. Same market, same invoice, no extra ad spend. At his close rate that was 3 extra deals in month 9 alone: $54K in profit against a $4,500 invoice.

### Sources you can cite

- **BASEO first-party data**: ONLY the Approved claims library in `baseo-context` § Claims policy. Never extrapolate beyond it.
- **Carrot** (State of Marketing report, blog data) — the dominant platform in the niche; the ICP trusts it.
- **Google official docs and announcements** (Search Central, update notes, AI Overviews docs).
- **Industry data**: ATTOM, NAR, RealtyTrac for deal/market economics; reputable SEO studies (Ahrefs, Semrush, Backlinko) for channel data.
- **AI vendor docs** (OpenAI, Anthropic, Perplexity) for crawler/citation mechanics.

If a sentence cannot be backed with a number, a quote, or a link, question whether the sentence is doing work. Cut, or back it up.

## Service integration (the most important rule)

Name it, prove the why, skip the how. BASEO appears **as the approach behind the result**, not as a sales pitch bolted to the end and not as an operations manual that hands competitors our process. There are three levels of detail, and the line between them is the whole rule:

1. **The WHAT and the WHY** (explain freely): the conceptual approach and the reason it works. "You need a unique page for every city you buy in, because Google's Scaled Content Abuse policy crushed swap-the-city-name templates." This teaches, demonstrates expertise, and is what makes the article rank and persuade. Go deep here.
2. **THAT we do it** (state plainly): "BASEO builds those city pages, installs the tracking, and rebuilds your Google Business Profile." A clear statement that BASEO handles it. This is the product placement.
3. **The proprietary HOW** (never publish): which call-tracking vendor, exactly how many pages or links per month, the internal QA and approval workflow, the specific AI-citation tooling, the link-outreach methodology. This NEVER goes on the blog. Mentioning that we do it is enough; readers will assume that's what should be done. Detailing how we do it only hands the playbook to competitors and dates the article when our stack changes. If a sentence seems to need a level-3 detail to land, that's the signal to rewrite the sentence at the WHAT/WHY level, not to add the detail.

### Good pattern (WHAT + WHY + that-we-do-it, no proprietary how)

> Most investor sites lose on one thing: the homepage is the only real page. On the sites that actually pull leads, the work sits in city pages and seller-situation pages, each one original to its market and its situation (probate, foreclosure, divorce, inherited, problem tenants). Template pages that just swap the city name got hit hard by Google's March 2024 update.
>
> That's the core of what BASEO builds, and we install the lead tracking first so every call and form is counted from day one. The order matters: situation pages without tracking is how your last agency hid for 8 months.

The reader understands the approach and why it works, and knows BASEO does it. He does not get a step-by-step operations manual.

### Too-much-detail pattern (pull it back)

> ❌ "We build 18 city pages in month one using [specific tool], run them through our 6-step internal QA checklist, then push 4 editorial links per month via our outreach SOP..."

Specific counts, named tools, and internal workflow. That's the proprietary how. Cut it to the WHAT/WHY/that-we-do-it level. The drafter never publishes level-3 detail and never leaves a placeholder for it: if a point can't be made without operational specifics, the point gets reframed, not flagged.

### Banned pattern (the empty name-drop)

> ❌ "There are many agencies that can help with this. One option is BASEO, which offers a range of SEO services for investors. Learn more at the end of this article."

That reduces BASEO to a footnote and sounds like every vendor. Delete it. Replace with the WHAT + WHY framing above.

### Frequency by funnel stage

Defined in `baseo-context` § Funnel stages. Summary:

- **TOFU**: 2–4 mentions, first before halfway point, soft audit CTA at end.
- **MOFU**: 4–8 mentions, the approach described is what BASEO does (WHAT + WHY, not the proprietary how), mid-post + end CTAs.
- **BOFU**: protagonist, named in nearly every section, hard CTA with multiple touches.

### Mention vs. empty name-drop

Don't confuse a clear statement of what BASEO does, framed with the why ("BASEO builds a unique page per market instead of templates, because Google penalizes duplicate city pages"), with a hollow name-drop ("BASEO can help with SEO"). The first persuades by showing you understand the problem. The second only reminds. Note: persuading does NOT require revealing the proprietary how — the why does the work.

### Process detail: never published

The proprietary how (tools, vendors, exact counts, internal workflow) never goes on the blog. The drafter writes at the WHAT/WHY/that-we-do-it level and stops there. `baseo-services-reference.md` defines what's safe to state about delivery; each service lists its "Off-blog detail (omit)" line so the line is explicit. If a draft drifts into operational specifics, the writer cuts them or reframes the sentence at the WHAT/WHY level. There is no placeholder and no exception: never invent delivery specifics, and never leave a level-3 detail (or a marker for one) in the article.

## Internal linking

### Density target

4–8 internal links per 1,000 words. Below 3 looks like an orphan post; above 10 looks stuffed.

### Mix per article

- **2–4 links to related blog posts** on the BASEO blog (from `baseo-corpus.csv`).
- **1–2 links to the audit/contact CTA or a verified case-study page** (per `baseo-context` § Canonical URLs).
- **1–2 external links to authoritative sources**: Google docs, Carrot data, original studies.

### Anchor text rules

- 2–6 words. Descriptive of the destination.
- Use the natural phrase, not the post title verbatim.
- Never "click here," "this article," "read more," "learn more."

### Where links go

In context, inside the sentence — not in a "related posts" list at the bottom.

## Visual cadence

The reader's eye should hit something visual roughly every 400–500 words. Mix of images, tables, callouts, and lists.

### Per-article minimums

- 2 images (SERP screenshots, GA4/Search Console charts, report excerpts, before/after traffic curves — real proof beats stock photos every time; per `baseo-context`, the ICP trusts raw data over polished design).
- 1 bulleted or numbered list.
- 1 table (channel comparison, deal-math breakdown, timeline, before/after).

### Encouraged additions

- 1–2 "What this means for your market" callouts.
- 1 pull quote or stat blockquote for the article's central number.

### Captions

Every image gets a caption that adds context. "Screenshot of Google Analytics" is not a caption. "The 'Direct' traffic spike in GA4 that turned out to be ChatGPT referrals" is a caption.

### Image generation prompts

Every image placeholder the drafter writes is a `[SCREENSHOT: ...]` block immediately followed by a `[PROMPT FOR CHATGPT IMAGE GENERATION: ...]` block (built from `baseo-image-prompts`). Both blocks are uploaded and visible in the WordPress draft. The publisher copies the prompt into ChatGPT, gets the image, and replaces both blocks with the real image in the editor (they're working-draft markers, removed once the real image is in). See `baseo-image-prompts` for categories (Google SERP, GBP/local, lead dashboard, hero, diagram) and templates.

## Tables and lists for SEO and readability

Tables and lists break the visual rhythm of prose AND capture Google's featured snippets. Each article should target at least ONE featured snippet type deliberately.

### Tables — when to use

- A comparison: 3+ items across 2+ attributes (channels by CPL and cost per deal; agency red flags vs what good looks like).
- Before/after states (traffic, leads, cost per lead by month).
- Timelines with multiple variables (month + work done + expected signal).

**Featured snippet pattern**: 4–8 rows, clear column headers, one idea per cell. Place the table near the H2 that introduces the topic.

### Numbered lists — when to use

Sequence matters: step-by-step playbooks, priority orders, month-by-month plans. 5–8 items, parallel structure, action-verb opens.

### Bulleted lists — when to use

Equivalent items, no order: red flags, checklist items, things to ask an agency. 5–8 items, one line each, no deep nesting.

### Anti-patterns

- Lists that should be tables (items with 2+ attributes each).
- Tables that should be lists (one attribute per item).
- Prose paragraphs hiding comma-separated lists.
- 2-item lists (use prose).
- Walls of bullets with no intro sentence.

### Targeting featured snippets explicitly

Before writing, check the SERP for the target keyword. If a snippet shows in position zero, match its format:

| If SERP shows | Structure your article so it has |
|---|---|
| Paragraph snippet | A 40–60 word definition/answer in the first paragraph after the intro |
| List snippet | An H2 dedicated to the list, surrounded by minimal prose |
| Table snippet | A clean comparison table with clear headers, placed above its explanation |

## Closes

Final section header: `## Final thoughts` (or `## The bottom line`, `## What to do next`). 100–200 words. One paragraph of takeaway, one of next-step.

### Banned closes

- "Hopefully this article helped you..."
- "If you found this helpful, share it!"
- "Subscribe to our newsletter for more tips."
- Pressure closes ("spots are filling fast").

### Recommended close pattern

1. One sentence restating the core insight (not summarizing every section).
2. One sentence on what to do next, in the reader's units (deals, leads, dollars).
3. The audit CTA, framed as zero-risk and consistent with the brand promise: free, written, no call required, yours to keep.

**Example**:
> The investors winning organic in your market aren't smarter. They started 18 months earlier and built the pages you haven't built yet.
>
> If you want to know exactly what's broken on your site and what your top competitor did instead, that's what the audit is for. Free, in writing, delivered in 2 business days. No call required. [Get your free site audit →](https://www.thebaseo.com/home-cash-buyers-seo/#hero)

### The expertise close (sanctioned — for playbook / MOFU articles)

After an article that lays out how the work is done, a confident "this is how we do it, and here's what it means for you" beat is allowed and effective. But it only works in the consultative form, where the reader draws the conclusion. The authority is anchored in **specialization** (we work only with cash home buyers, so we already know your market), not in self-praise.

**Good (consultative — reader concludes):**
> That's the whole playbook, and every piece of it is work, every month. Some operators run it themselves and do fine. Most would rather spend that time closing deals. If you want to know which pieces your site is missing before you decide either way, that's what the audit is for. We work only with cash home buyers, so it already knows your competitors, your keywords, and your seller situations. Free, in writing, no call. [Get your free site audit →](https://www.thebaseo.com/home-cash-buyers-seo/#hero)

What makes it work: it offers the reader the out ("some run it themselves and do fine"), which is the confidence move this burned ICP respects, and it proves expertise by specialization rather than claiming it.

**Bad (arrogant — triggers the burned-investor allergy):**
> ❌ "That's how we do it. If your setup doesn't look like this, you're with the wrong team. It's why we're the experts in cash home buyers and why investors come to us."

"Wrong team," "we're the experts," "come to us" is exactly the agency arrogance the ICP distrusts (see `baseo-context` § Banned trust-killers). It claims authority instead of proving it and pushes instead of letting the reader decide.

### Frequency cap on the expertise nudge

The "this is how we do it / here's the gap you have" self-referential nudge appears **at most once or twice per article, ideally once, in the close**. Three or more times reads as self-promotion and pulls the article back toward the agency-pitch tone the ICP rejects. The cap is on this specific nudge, not on BASEO mentions overall (which follow the funnel-stage frequency). When in doubt, use it once, at the end.

## Anti-patterns

The full list of banned clichés, agency-speak, trust-killers, structural patterns, and anti-angles lives in `baseo-context` (§ Banned clichés and § Anti-angles). Read those sections before drafting and search your draft for each banned phrase before submitting.

Three or more banned phrases in a draft → automatic return for revision. The intro-specific banned openers count toward this total.

## Quick checklist before submitting

- Intro is under 80 words and uses Template A, B, or C.
- H2 keyword coverage ≥ 60%.
- Most paragraphs are 1–3 sentences.
- At least 3 sentences carry a specific number with a source; at least one converts to deal math.
- Every first-party result claim traces to the Approved claims library.
- BASEO appears as the approach (WHAT + WHY + that-we-do-it) at least once, not just a CTA and not as a proprietary operations manual, per the brief's framing.
- No package names, no BASEO prices, no ranking or lead guarantees.
- 4–8 internal links per 1,000 words, descriptive anchors, pulled from `baseo-corpus.csv`.
- At least 2 images + 1 list + 1 table.
- No banned phrases, no agency-speak left untranslated.
- Reads like an operator wrote it — do the B1 read-through (Voice rule 9).
- The expertise nudge ("this is how we do it / here's your gap") appears at most 1–2 times, ideally once in the close, and in the consultative form (never "wrong team / we're the experts").
- Final thoughts section is 100–200 words and ends with the audit CTA.
