### H2: What Is Real Estate Wholesaling?
- **Word target**: 130–160
- **Sub-bullets**:
  1. Plain-language middleman model: find a motivated seller, get the house under contract at a discount, assign that contract to a cash buyer, collect the difference.
  2. The load-bearing distinction: the wholesaler NEVER owns the property. You control it with a contract and sell the contract, not the house.
  3. Translate the jargon at first use: equitable interest (the right to buy that the contract gives you) and assignment (transferring that right to another buyer).

### H2: Is Wholesaling Real Estate Legal?
- **Word target**: 130–150
- **Sub-bullets**:
  1. **Snippet answer (first 2 lines, 40–55 words)**: Yes, wholesaling is legal in all 50 states. You're selling your rights to a purchase contract, not the property itself. But several states tightened the rules in 2026, so what's legal depends on how you market the deal and where you operate.
  2. The legal line: marketing the CONTRACT (your equitable interest) is generally fine; marketing the PROPERTY itself without a license looks like unlicensed brokerage and is where wholesalers get in trouble.
  3. Caveat hard: this is general information, not legal advice. Verify current rules with a licensed real estate attorney or your state real estate commission before your first deal.
- **Data**: "legal across the country; South Carolina the only arguable exception" ([Real Estate Bees](https://realestatebees.com/statistics/is-wholesaling-real-estate-legal/))
- **BASEO reference**: none
- **Snippet capture**: yes — paragraph (40–55 words) at the very top

#### H3: States With Strict or New Wholesaling Laws
- **Word target**: 100–140
- **Sub-bullets**:
  1. South Carolina — HB 4754 defines wholesaling as brokerage; you need a license. The hardest state to wholesale legally.
  2. Illinois — dealing in real estate contracts (including assignable contracts) on 2+ occasions in any 12-month period makes you a "broker," so unlicensed wholesalers are effectively limited to about one deal per year.
  3. Pennsylvania — Act 52 now regulates wholesaling with registration and disclosure requirements.
  4. The trend: six new wholesaling laws passed in 2025 across five states (Connecticut, Maryland, North Dakota, Oklahoma, Tennessee), mostly disclosure/registration rules; roughly a dozen states now regulate it in some form.
  5. Repeat the caveat: check your own state, rules are changing fast, talk to an attorney.
- **Data**: [Real Estate Bees](https://realestatebees.com/statistics/is-wholesaling-real-estate-legal/) (SC/IL/PA) + [Leonine Public Affairs](https://leoninepublicaffairs.com/new-state-laws-for-real-estate-wholesaling-in-2025/) (2025 states)

#### H3: Step 4: Analyze the Deal (ARV & MAO)
- **Word target**: 130–160
- **Sub-bullets**:
  1. ARV (After Repair Value) = what the house is worth fully fixed, estimated from comparable sold homes.
  2. MAO (Maximum Allowable Offer) = the most you can offer and still leave room for everyone.
  3. The 70% rule formula: MAO = (ARV × 0.70) − repair costs − your wholesale fee.
  4. Worked example: $200,000 ARV, $30,000 repairs, $10,000 fee → MAO = ($200,000 × 0.70) − $30,000 − $10,000 = $100,000.
  5. Why the 30% buffer: it covers the flipper's profit, holding costs, closing costs, and surprises, which is what makes the deal attractive to your cash buyer.
- **Data**: MAO formula + rationale ([Real Estate Skills](https://www.realestateskills.com/blog/wholesale-formula))
- **Visual element**: callout or inline formula box (the MAO line) — also snippet-friendly
- **Snippet capture**: optional secondary (formula line)

#### H3: Step 5: Get the Property Under Contract
- **Word target**: 100–130
- **Sub-bullets**:
  1. Use a purchase agreement with an assignment clause (so you can transfer it) and an inspection/exit contingency (so you can walk if you can't place it).
  2. Put down earnest money to make the contract real, usually a few hundred dollars.
  3. Use a contract template reviewed by a local real estate attorney — do not download a random form and wing it.
- **Watch out for**: presenting a template as legal advice; add the "attorney-reviewed" caveat
- **Snippet capture**: no

#### H3: Step 6: Build Your Cash Buyers List
- **Word target**: 110–140
- **Sub-bullets**:
  1. Have buyers lined up before or right after you sign, not after; a contract you can't assign is a dead deal.
  2. Where to find them: investor-friendly agents, local REIA meetings, county records of recent cash purchases, and active real estate investor Facebook groups.
  3. Qualify them: what they buy, which areas, price range, and how fast they close. A short list of real cash buyers beats a long list of tire-kickers.

#### H3: Step 7: Assign the Contract and Close
- **Word target**: 100–130

  1. Sign an assignment agreement transferring your contract to the cash buyer for your fee.
  2. Close at a title company or with a real estate attorney who handles assignments; they disburse your fee at closing.
  3. Use DocuSign or similar to move signatures fast, motivated sellers cool off when things drag.

### H2: How Much Can You Make Wholesaling Real Estate?
- **Word target**: 130–160

  1. **Snippet answer up top (40–55 words)**: A typical assignment fee runs $5,000–$15,000, and the national average is about $13,000. In most markets $8,000–$12,000 is the sweet spot; hot, high-value markets reach $15,000–$25,000+. First-year wholesalers often average $5,000–$10,000 per deal.
