## Angle / POV

Not a generic consumer "how to buy a foreclosure" rehash. It's the same stage-by-stage answer written for an operator who thinks in deals and cost of capital: cash is only *required* at the auction, and the reason is mechanical (lenders can't underwrite in 24–72 hours), so the smart move for most investors is to finance the financeable stages and reserve cash (or a close-like-cash tool) for the auctions that actually demand it. #### H3: Pre-Foreclosure and Short Sales
- Owner still holds title, sells before losing it; short sale = less than owed, lender must approve. Financeable with conventional or government loans. Slow because of lender approval. Investor note: months of lender approval ties up nothing but time — cash isn't the constraint here.

#### H3: Bank-Owned (REO) Properties
- The most "normal" financeable path. Define REO (didn't sell at auction, back to the bank). Livable REO buys with conventional/FHA/VA like any house. Sold as-is, so condition/appraisal can gate the loan. Investor note: get preapproved with the REO's own listing lender expectations in mind.

#### H3: Foreclosure Auctions
- The cash exception. Sheriff's/trustee sale: cash or certified funds, close in 24–72h, no inspection. This is why the typical operator shouldn't *start* at the auction. Links forward to the two next sections.

### H2: Why Auctions Usually Require Cash
- **Original guideline**: The mechanical "why" competitors gloss. Lenders can't fund without inspection, appraisal, clear title; 24–72h close is incompatible with 30–45 day underwriting. Result: certified funds. Educational tone. 110–150 words.
- **Persona angle**: this is the operator's "aha" — it's not a rule someone invented to keep you out, it's underwriting math. Once you get it, you know exactly which deals need cash.
- **Claims to use**: auction close 24–72h + no inspection/appraisal/title (auction.com, Rocket Mortgage); underwriting 30–45 days (Rocket Mortgage 203k page contrast).
- **Snippet capture**: no.
- **Watch out for**: making it sound like a conspiracy; keep it mechanical.

### H2: How to Finance a Foreclosure Without Cash
- **Original guideline**: Intro paragraph presenting loan options for the financeable stages. Preview the 4 H3s. 50–70 words.
- **Persona angle**: frame as "keep your capital working across more deals." Flag up front that two of these four are owner-occupant-only.
- **Snippet capture**: no.

#### H3: Conventional Loans
- Standard for livable REO/short sales. Preapproval = step 1. Home must be insurable/lendable condition. Typical down payment. Keyword: conventional mortgage foreclosure.

#### H3: FHA Loans (3.5% Down)
- Low-cash path. 3.5% down at 580+; 10% at 500–579; must meet FHA minimum property standards; **primary residence only, not investors/flippers** (honest caveat). Sources: Rocket Mortgage, Policygenius.

#### H3: FHA 203(k) Renovation Loans
- Differentiator: purchase + repairs in one loan, built for beat-up foreclosures. Limited 203(k) up to $75,000, non-structural; Standard 203(k) min $5,000 + HUD consultant, structural; licensed contractor; work starts within 30 days of closing; **primary residence only** (honest caveat — not a flip vehicle). Sources: Rocket Mortgage, HUD.

#### H3: Hard Money and HELOCs
- The investor's actual tools. Hard money: fast, short-term, asset-based, high rates, down payments up to ~30% — and the "close like cash" at auction then refinance play. HELOC: pull equity from another property. Warn on cost. Sources: Policygenius.
