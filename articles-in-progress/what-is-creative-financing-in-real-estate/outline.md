#### H3: Seller Financing (Owner Financing)
- **Word target**: 130–160
- Seller is the bank; down payment commonly 10–20% (some want 25%+), interest ~5–10%, term 3–30 yrs often with a balloon. Buyer benefit: no bank underwriting, faster close, negotiable terms. Buyer risk: balloon comes due, possibly higher rate than a bank.
- Data: "10–20% down, ~5–10% interest, balloon common" — [Nav](https://www.nav.com/blog/owner-financing-307751/) / [Forbes Advisor](https://www.forbes.com/advisor/mortgages/owner-financing/)

#### H3: Subject-To Financing
- **Word target**: 150–180
- Buyer takes over existing mortgage payments; loan stays in the seller's name; title transfers to the buyer. Upside: acquire with almost no cash, inherit the seller's rate. Real risk (concrete): the due-on-sale clause lets the lender call the full balance when title transfers without consent.
- Data: due-on-sale / Garn-St Germain (12 U.S.C. 1701j-3) — [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Due-on-sale_clause)
- Internal link: "pre-foreclosure" → https://www.thebaseo.com/home-cash-buyers-seo/resources/what-pre-foreclosure-means-for-buyers/

#### H3: Lease Options (Rent-to-Own)
- **Word target**: 110–140
- Control the property now, buy later at a set price; upfront option fee + a rent credit toward purchase. Fits low-down control plays. Trade-off: you may never exercise; disputes if terms are loose.

#### H3: Loan Assumption
- **Word target**: 95–120
- Buyer formally takes over the seller's mortgage with lender approval, which releases the seller from liability (the key difference vs subject-to). Mostly FHA/VA assumable loans; attractive when the existing rate is below market.

#### H3: Hard Money & Private Lending
- **Word target**: 95–120
- Short-term, asset-based loans from private lenders/individuals; fast but expensive (higher rate + points). Fits flips/bridge, not long holds.
- Internal link: "wholesaling" already used; optionally none.

#### H3: Wraparound Mortgages
- **Word target**: 95–120
- Seller keeps the original loan and writes a new, larger loan that "wraps around" it; buyer pays the seller, seller keeps paying the underlying note. Advanced; same due-on-sale exposure.

### H2: Is Creative Financing Legal?

- **Word target**: 150–180
- **Sub-bullets**:
  1. Answer first sentence: yes, generally legal, but regulated and state-dependent.
  2. Dodd-Frank/SAFE Act: financing ≤3 properties/12 months to an owner-occupant (1–4 units) is exempt from MLO licensing; above that, ability-to-repay underwriting applies. Investor-to-investor and non-owner-occupied deals differ.
  3. Subject-to/wraps carry the due-on-sale exposure; private deals have fewer consumer protections.
  4. Caveat: use a real estate attorney and title company. Not legal advice.
- Data: [NAR — SAFE Act](https://www.nar.realtor/the-safe-act-seller-financing)
- **Visual element**: none. **Snippet**: no.

### H2: Frequently Asked Questions

- **Word target**: 40–55 words per answer.

#### H3: Is creative financing a good idea for investors?
- Answer: good when you have deal flow but limited capital or can't/won't use a bank; it stretches cash across more doors and moves fast. But it adds risk and complexity, so it fits operators who understand the terms and use an attorney.

#### H3: What is the most common type of creative financing?
- Answer: seller (owner) financing. The seller acts as the lender: the buyer puts money down, then repays the balance in installments with interest over an agreed term. It's popular because it's flexible and skips bank underwriting.

#### H3: Do you need good credit for creative financing?
- Answer: usually not. Terms are set with the seller or a private lender, not a bank, so credit matters less than the deal and the down payment. The seller sets the bar, which is typically more flexible than a lender's.

#### H3: What are the risks of subject-to financing for the buyer?
- Answer: the loan stays in the seller's name, so you depend on them and their servicer; and the due-on-sale clause lets the lender demand full repayment when title transfers. Get legal and title guidance before closing one.

#### H3: Is seller financing legal?
- Answer: yes, but it's regulated. Dodd-Frank and the SAFE Act limit how many owner-occupied homes you can seller-finance before licensing/underwriting rules kick in, and terms must be documented. Use a real estate attorney and title company.
