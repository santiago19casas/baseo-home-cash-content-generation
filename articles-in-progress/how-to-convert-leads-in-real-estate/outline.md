#### H3: How to calculate your own lead-to-deal ratio

- **Word target**: 130–160
- **Sub-bullets**:
  1. The formula: (deals closed ÷ leads received) × 100. Run it per source, never in aggregate. Aggregate hides which channel to shut off.
  2. The calculation that actually moves money: cost per deal = marketing spend ÷ deals closed. Compare it to your average spread.
     - Data: average wholesale assignment fee is $13,000 across a survey of 1,000+ wholesalers, from about $5,000 in Arizona to about $22,000 in North Carolina and Georgia — [Real Estate Bees](https://realestatebees.com/statistics/average-wholesale-assignment-fee/)
  3. **Worked example, round numbers** (must not land on any BASEO package price — avoid $2,500 / $4,500 / $7,500 / $1,500 as outputs):
     - 400 leads in a quarter from one paid source. 4 contracts. 2 closed. Lead-to-contract: 1%.
     - $12,000 spent on that source. Cost per deal: $6,000.
     - Against a $13,000 average assignment fee, that channel nets about $7,000 a deal before anything else.
     - Now run the same math on referrals: 20 leads, 3 closed, $0 spent.
     - The point: the second channel is smaller and better, and only the per-source math shows you that.

### H2: Why most real estate leads never convert

- **Word target**: 170–200

### H2: Step 1: Win the first 5 minutes (speed to lead)

- **Word target**: 200–250
- **Sub-bullets**:
  1. The one number to remember. Open with it.
     - Data: contacting a lead within 5 minutes instead of 30 makes you **21x more likely to qualify** that lead, and 100x more likely to reach them at all. MIT / InsideSales, Dr. James Oldroyd, 15,000+ leads and 100,000+ dials — [source](https://www.revenue.io/inside-sales-glossary/what-is-lead-response-time). **Credit MIT/InsideSales, never Harvard.** State the study is from 2007 and note it has never been dislodged; the honesty reads better to this reader than hiding the date.
  2. Optional supporting stat: 78% of buyers purchase from whoever responds first (Lead Connect, as reported by [LeadResponse](https://leadresponse.co/blog/speed-to-lead-statistics)). Attribute explicitly or cut. Not load-bearing.
  3. Why it hits harder for motivated sellers than for agents: a distressed seller isn't shopping five offers over three weeks. He's trying to make a problem stop. He signs with whoever shows up while the problem is still on his mind. Speed beats price more often than investors want to believe.
  4. **Translate to actions** (this is the operator payoff, keep it concrete):
     - Call first. Not email. Email is a message; a call is a conversation.
     - Double touch: call, and if no answer, text within 60 seconds. "Hi [name], it's [you], you just asked about an offer on [address]. Calling you back now."
     - Push notifications on the form fill, on the phone in your pocket. Not an email digest.
     - Decide today who answers after 6pm, and put it in writing. Most seller form fills don't land at 10am on a Tuesday.
     - The 2am lead: auto-text on submission so the seller knows he's been heard, then a live call first thing. The auto-text buys you the night. It does not replace the call.
  5. Keyword: "speed to lead" must appear naturally, ideally in the first sentence of the action block.
- **BASEO reference**: **none.** Auto-SMS and CRM notifications are out-of-scope services. This section teaches the practice and never implies BASEO installs it.
- **Visual element**: image — `[SCREENSHOT: ...]` Category 3, a lead-report view with a **speed-to-first-contact** column, showing the contrast between leads answered in minutes and leads answered in hours.

#### H3: The 6 questions that separate a deal from a tire-kicker

- **Word target**: 210–250
- **Structure**: numbered list of 6 literal questions. Under each, one italic line: *What you're actually listening for.* Copyable, screenshot-able. This is the tactical gap in the SERP.
  1. "Why are you selling now, and why now instead of six months ago?" — *listening for: the event. Foreclosure notice, a death, a divorce filing, a tenant who trashed the place. No event usually means no deal yet.*
  2. "If this doesn't sell in the next 60 days, what happens?" — *listening for: whether there's a consequence. A seller with no consequence has no deadline, and a seller with no deadline is a nurture lead, not a deal.*
  3. "What's still owed on it?" — *listening for: whether the math can even work. If the payoff is above what the house supports, you're done in 30 seconds instead of 30 days.*
  4. "If you were keeping it, what would you fix first?" — *listening for: the real condition, in his words, without putting him on the defensive. Sellers understate damage when you ask "what's wrong with it." They tell the truth when you ask what they'd fix.*
  5. "Have you had other investors out to look at it?" — *listening for: whether you're first, fifth, or last. It also tells you what he's already been offered and what he turned down.*
  6. "If I could close in two weeks and you didn't spend a dollar on repairs, what number would get you to sign this week?" — *listening for: his real number, in his own mouth, before you say yours. Whoever names the number first sets the frame.*
- **Internal links**: "questions to ask motivated sellers" → https://www.thebaseo.com/home-cash-buyers-seo/resources/questions-to-ask-motivated-sellers/
- **BASEO reference**: none
- **Visual element**: list

### H2: Step 3: Run the offer conversation, not a sales pitch

- **Word target**: 150–180
- **Sub-bullets**:
  1. Thesis: the seller isn't comparing your offer to another offer. He's comparing it to the number Zillow told him his house is worth. That number has no repairs, no commission and no carrying costs in it.
  2. So the job is not defending your number. It's changing what the number gets compared to: cash, a close in about two weeks, as-is condition, no commission, no showings, no repairs, versus a retail listing.
  3. Build the comparison as **net to seller**, not price. Retail price is a headline. Net to seller is what lands in his account.
  4. Keywords to place naturally: cash offer, ARV, as-is, net to seller.
- **Table** (clearly labeled as a worked example, not market data):

| | List with an agent | Your cash offer |
|---|---|---|
| Headline price | $200,000 | $130,000 |
| Agent commission (about 6%) | −$12,000 | $0 |
| Repairs to make it listable | −$20,000 | $0 |
| Carrying costs, 60–90 days on market | −$4,000 | $0 |
| Closing costs and concessions | −$6,000 | Typically covered |
| **Net to seller** | **about $158,000, in 3+ months, if it appraises** | **$130,000, in about 2 weeks, guaranteed to fund** |

- **Interpretation**: the gap is real and you should say so out loud. You're not pretending $130,000 equals $158,000. You're showing him what the $158,000 actually costs him: three months, a contractor, and a buyer whose loan can still fall through. Some sellers want the retail number and should go list. The ones who want the problem gone today are yours.
- **BASEO reference**: none
- **Visual element**: table
- **Watch out for**: the word "guaranteed" appears in the table cell about funding. That is a statement about a cash close, not a marketing guarantee, but self-check will flag the string. **Rewrite the cell as "$130,000, in about 2 weeks, no financing to fall through"** to avoid the collision. Use that version.

#### H3: How to anchor price without killing trust

- **Word target**: 130–160
- **Sub-bullets**:
  1. Give the range before the number. "Based on what you've told me, I'm probably in the $125,000 to $135,000 range. Let me see the place and I'll firm it up." A range invites a conversation. A single number invites a yes or a no.
  2. Explain the formula out loud. ARV × 70%, minus repairs, is the standard starting point, and there is nothing to hide in it. A seller who understands *why* the number is the number argues with it far less than a seller who thinks you pulled it out of the air. Frame it as the industry's standard starting formula, not a law.
  3. Worked example, $200,000 ARV: 70% is $140,000. Minus $20,000 in repairs, you're at $120,000. Say that math to him. Out loud.
  4. Never raise the price for free. If you go up, something comes back: a faster close, the furniture stays, the inspection contingency comes off. Free concessions teach him the first number was fake.
  5. The deal killer: dropping the price after the inspection with no warning. It kills more deals than a low opening number, because it turns a negotiation into a bait and switch. If a re-trade is possible, say so on day one.
- **BASEO reference**: none
- **Watch out for**: stating the 70% rule as universal law

#### H3: Handling the three objections that kill cash deals

- **Word target**: 170–200
- **Structure**: three blocks. Objection (bold) → *What it actually means* → the model response, written as literal words the investor can say.
  1. **"Your offer is too low."** — *He's comparing your price to a retail headline, not to his net.* Response: "It's lower than the Zillow number, and it should be. Let me show you what the Zillow number actually pays out after commission, repairs and three months of holding it. If the retail net is better for you, I'll tell you to go list it."
  2. **"I need to think about it / talk to my family."** — *Either there's no urgency, or the person who decides isn't in the room.* Response: "That makes sense. Who else is part of this decision? Let's get them on the phone together, because I'd rather answer their questions than have you try to." Never push past this one. Find the missing decision-maker instead.
  3. **"I got a higher offer from another investor."** — *He may have. Whether it closes is a different question.* Response: "That's worth taking, if it closes. Ask them two things: is there an inspection contingency, and does the price change after they walk it? A lot of high offers come down after the inspection. Mine won't, and I'll put that in writing." Teach the reader the distinction: an offer that gets signed and an offer that gets honored are not the same product.
- **BASEO reference**: none
- **Watch out for**: naming or trashing real competitors; the word "guarantee"

### H2: Step 4: Build a follow-up cadence that closes deals 60 days later

- **Word target**: 140–170
- **Sub-bullets**:
  1. **Rebuilt anchor** (the "touch 6 to 10" statistic did not survive verification — see dossier). Write it as: sellers almost never say yes on the first or second contact. Direct-mail operators put the average at around six touches before a prospect responds at all, and Goliath's motivated-seller data says the same thing in different words: sellers convert after repeated follow-up, not after the first call ([Goliath Data](https://goliathdata.com/motivated-seller-leads-conversion-rate)). Most investors stop at two.
  2. The money frame, and it's the strongest sentence in the section: **that lead is already paid for.** You spent the money the day it came in. Following up costs nothing but the calendar reminder. It is the only channel in the business with zero marginal cost.
  3. The 60-day deal is not a slow deal. It's a deal that was never available in week one, because the seller wasn't ready in week one. The auction date moved. The tenant left. The sibling agreed. You want to be the investor whose name is on the calendar when that happens.

#### H3: A 90-day cadence you can copy

| Day | Touch | What it carries |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Call within 5 minutes, text if no answer | You exist, you're real, you're fast |

#### H3: Cadence by seller type (pre-foreclosure, probate, tired landlord)

- **Word target**: 170–200
- **Structure**: four short blocks, each with its trigger, its clock, and its tone.
  - **Pre-foreclosure**: the auction date is the deadline, and it's public. Cadence tightens as it approaches: every 5 days at 90 days out, more often inside 30. Tone: calm and specific. He is being called by everyone, and most of them sound like a scam.
  - **Probate / inherited**: the clock belongs to the court, and to grief. 30-day cadence, educational, patient. The heir often doesn't know what he's allowed to do yet. The investor who explained the process is the one who gets the call when the estate clears.
  - **Tired landlord**: no deadline at all, which is why most investors drop him. The trigger is external: the next bad tenant, the next $8,000 repair, the next eviction. Long nurture, low frequency, and be the first call when the water heater goes.
  - **Divorce**: two decision-makers who don't agree, and absolute neutrality is the job. Never take a side, never relay a message between them, always communicate with both at once. A deal that looks dead can close the week the settlement lands.
- **Internal links**: "how the pre-foreclosure timeline works" → https://www.thebaseo.com/home-cash-buyers-seo/resources/what-pre-foreclosure-means-for-buyers/

- **Watch out for**: any tone that treats distress as an opportunity to exploit. Dignified, operator-to-operator.

### H2: Step 6: Convert leads you never talked to (retargeting and re-engagement)

- **Word target**: 140–170
- **Sub-bullets**:
  1. Most motivated sellers don't decide on the first interaction. They keep looking, for weeks, while the problem gets worse. Retargeting on Google, Facebook and Instagram is what keeps your name in front of a seller who isn't ready yet and will be.
  2. The cheaper play nobody runs: **reactivate the dead list.** Every investor has a folder of sellers who said no thanks 6 to 12 months ago. A chunk of them listed with an agent, sat, expired, and are still stuck with the same house and less patience.
  3. The concrete campaign, written as a one-line text: "Hi [name], [you] here. We spoke last spring about [address]. Did it end up selling? If not, I'm still buying in the area and my offer would look different today." One line, no pitch, easy to answer. Send it to the whole dead list on a Tuesday morning and work whatever comes back.
  4. It costs nothing and the list is already yours. Compare that to the cost of a fresh lead from the same market.
- **BASEO reference**: **none.** BASEO manages PPC, but does not sell social retargeting or SMS-as-a-service. Teach the play, don't claim it.

### H2: The conversion multiplier nobody talks about: where the lead came from

- **Word target**: 200–240
- **Sub-bullets**:
  1. The honest close to the playbook: you can run every step above perfectly and still lose, because a lead that was sold to five investors was a race you entered late. Speed doesn't fix that. Scripts don't fix that. It's a source problem.
  2. The contrast, using the numbers already established up top: a portal lead converts at 0.4% to 1.2% and arrives with four other investors already dialing. A referral converts at 14% to 30% because the seller arrived pre-sold. An inbound organic seller who typed "sell my house fast [city]" into Google and called the first result behaves like a referral. He qualified himself, he chose you, and nobody else is on the phone.
  3. The economics: a paid lead costs the same today as it did last year, or more. An organic lead gets cheaper every month the page keeps ranking, because the page is paid for.
     - Data (first-party, verbatim from the Approved claims library): one Florida cash buyer went from **3 to 28 motivated seller leads a month in nine months**, same market, no extra ad spend, at **$161 per organic lead and falling** (BASEO client data).
  4. **The 2026 layer (B5).** More sellers now open ChatGPT and ask who buys houses for cash in their city, and they act on the answer. Traffic arriving from ChatGPT converts at **15.9%, against 1.76% for Google organic** ([Seer Interactive](https://www.seerinteractive.com/insights/case-study-6-learnings-about-how-traffic-from-chatgpt-converts)) — because the seller finished making his decision inside the conversation before he ever clicked.
